\section{Aim}
The aim of the experiment is to measure the magnetic susceptibility of the salt solution based on the principle that in a non-uniform magnetic field, magnetic dipoles within the liquid align with the field. This creates a force that pulls the liquid into the region of the stronger field (between the magnet poles), causing the liquid level to rise against gravity.

\section{Theory}
When an object is placed in a magnetic field, a magnetic moment is induced in it. The magnetic moment can then be measured by measuring the force exerted by the sample in an inhomogeneous magnetic field. The force on the sample is  negative of the gradient of the change in energy density when the sample is placed. And a simplified expression can be written as:
$$ \chi = \frac{2 \rho g h}{\mu_0 H^2} $$

This quantity $\chi$ can be referred to as Volume Magnetic susceptibility, to calculate mass susceptibility and molar susceptibility. We need to use the following formulae:
\begin{align*}
	\chi^{\prime}_{mass} &= \chi/\rho \\
\chi^{\prime\prime}_{molar} &= M\chi^{\prime}
\end{align*}

Water is a diamagnetic and we can remove its contribution from the volume susceptibility to calculate the mass susceptibility for just the salt. 
\begin{align*}
	\chi_{sol} &= \chi_{salt} + \chi_{water} \\ 
\end{align*}

For Solution - II,
\begin{align*}
	\SI{3.7486e-4}{} &= \chi_{salt} - (\SI{9.04e-6}{}) \\ 
	\chi_{salt} &= \SI{3.8390e-4}{}
\end{align*}

Similar relation can be written about the mass susceptibility of the solution:
\begin{align*}
	\chi^{\prime}_{sol} &= \frac{m_{salt}}{m_{sol}}\chi^{\prime}_{salt} + \frac{m_{water}}{m_{sol}}\chi^{\prime}_{water} \\ 
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
	\chi^{\prime}_{sol} &= \frac{7.5}{22.46}\chi^{\prime}_{salt} + \frac{14.96}{22.46}(\SI{- 9.04e-9}{}) \\
	\frac{\SI{3.7486e-4}{}}{1280} &= 0.3339\chi^{\prime}_{salt} - (\SI{6.02e-9}{}) \\ 
	\chi^{\prime}_{salt} &=  \SI{8.9511e-7}{}
\end{align*}

Now, if we were to calculate the Molar susceptibility we get:
\begin{align*}
	\chi_{molar} &= \SI{169.02e-3}{} \times \SI{8.9511e-7}{} \\
	\chi_{molar} &= \SI{1.5127e-7}{}
\end{align*}

\subsection*{Error Analysis}
\begin{align*}
	\textrm{Error in Molar Susceptibility result} &= \frac{\SI{1.7844e-7}{}-\SI{1.5127e-7}{}}{\SI{1.7844e-7}{}}\times 100\% \\
						      &= 15.22\%
\end{align*}

The propagation of uncertainty while taking measurement can be written as:

\begin{align*}
	\frac{ \sigma_{\chi^{\prime\prime}} }{ \chi^{\prime\prime} } &=  \frac{ \sigma_{\chi^{\prime}} }{ \chi^{\prime} } \\
								     &= \sqrt{ \left(\frac{0.1}{7.5}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{0.1}{22.46}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{ \sigma_{\chi^{\prime}_{sol}}}{ \chi^{\prime}_{sol} }\right)^2 } \\
								     &= \sqrt{ \left(\frac{0.1}{7.5}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{0.1}{22.46}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\sigma_{\chi}}{\chi} \right)^2 } \\
								     &= \sqrt{ \left(\frac{0.1}{7.5}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{0.1}{22.46}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\sigma_{slope}}{\textrm{slope}} \right)^2 } \\
	\sigma_{\chi^{\prime\prime}} &= \SI{1.5127e-7}{} \sqrt{ \left(\frac{0.1}{7.5}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{0.1}{22.46}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\slopeErrSolTwoDec}{\slopeSolTwoDec} \right)^2 } \\
	\sigma_{\chi^{\prime\prime}} &= \SI{7.68e-9}{} 
\end{align*}

This uncertainty due to measurment comes around to be about 5\%. Using this we can safely say that our experiment was able to measure the magnetic susceptibilty of the salt under certain standard deviation limits.

\textbf{Why is our result slightly lower than theoritcal?} \\
We attribute this deviation to be mostly arising from a improper setup. Before our second set of readings, we tried to make sure our tube was clean, and used IPA to help us aid in that process. We waited for long enough time for the IPA to dry off, but as we started taking readings for our second solution we see that some bubbles are rising in the tube, especially at the thinner end. This was obvious to see through the microscope, and minorly affected our ability to take the microscope reading. In hindsight, we realised that this must be the IPA rising as it is lighter compared to our solution. This introduced a IPA which slightly diluted our solution and affected the density value. Magnetically this doesn't had a significant as it is dimagnetic like water, but has even lesser magnetic moment. In an extreme case, oxidation of manganese ions in the solution could also have played a role in the decreased value. Also the theoritcal value is temperature dependent and measured at \SI{293}{\kelvin} which is definitly lower than the temperature setting for our experiment. And a higher temperature would lead to a lower value as the magnetization is related to temperature as inverse dependency as stated by Curie's Law.

We successfully completed the experiment by measuring the magnetic susceptibility of the salt, manganese sufate monohydrate. Our measured value for the molar magnetic susceptibility \textbf{($\SI{1.5217e-7}{}$)} was about \textbf{~15\%} deviation form the theoritical value.

Our value being lower than the theoritical one can be easily explained because of the uncertainty in the solution density and temperature conditions.
