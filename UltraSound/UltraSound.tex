\section{Aim}
To study the diffraction of light by ultrasonic waves and to determine the wavelength and velocity of ultrasonic waves in a liquid using a piezoelectric crystal driven by both sine and square wave signals.

\section{Theory}
When ultrasonic waves generated by a transducer travel down a liquid medium and reflect at the bottom, they interfere to form a stationary or standing wave pattern. The compressions and rarefactions of this acoustic standing wave act as a dynamic optical grating, diffracting a laser beam passing perpendicularly through it.

The velocity of the ultrasonic wave ($V$) in the liquid is determined by the fundamental relation:
$$V = f \Lambda$$
where $f$ is the frequency of the crystal oscillator and $\Lambda$ is the wavelength of the ultrasonic waves.

By utilizing the principles of diffraction, the ultrasonic wavelength $\Lambda$ can be deduced using:
$$\Lambda = \frac{n \lambda}{\sin \theta_n}$$
where $n$ is the order of diffraction, $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the incident laser, and $\theta_n$ is the angle of diffraction for the $n^{\text{th}}$ order spot.

The angle of diffraction $\theta_n$ is geometrically calculated as:
$$\theta_n = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{D_n}{L}\right)$$
where $D_n$ is the linear distance from the central bright spot ($0^{\text{th}}$ order) to the $n^{\text{th}}$ order spot, and $L$ is the distance measured from the crystal oscillator to the screen/detector.

\section{Experimental Observation}
During the experiment, the positions of the diffraction spots were measured using a traveling microscope equipped with a least count of 0.01 mm. Measurements were recorded twice: initially utilizing a sine wave signal, and subsequently using a square wave signal to drive the piezoelectric crystal.

The relative distance of each diffraction spot, $D_n$, was evaluated by subtracting the coordinate of the $0^{\text{th}}$ order central spot ($x_0$) from the coordinate of the $n^{\text{th}}$ order spot ($x_n$), resulting in $D_n = |x_n - x_0|$. The diffraction angle $\theta_n$ for each order was then computed geometrically using the fixed detector distance, $L = \detectorDist$.

For each individual order, the ultrasonic wavelength $\Lambda$ was calculated using the given laser wavelength $\lambda = \laserWavelength$ and the respective $\theta_n$. Finally, the ultrasonic velocity $V$ was calculated using the operational crystal frequency $f = \crystalFreq$.

To improve the precision of our estimation and mitigate isolated measurement errors, we utilized a linear regression fit mapping the spot distance $D_n$ against the diffraction order $n$. Because the angle $\theta_n$ is exceedingly small, the small-angle approximation ($\sin \theta \approx \tan \theta \approx D_n / L$) applies gracefully. This simplifies the relationship to:
$$D_n = \left(\frac{\lambda L}{\Lambda}\right) n$$

Plotting $D_n$ vs. $n$ yields a straight line passing through the origin, where the slope $m = \frac{\lambda L}{\Lambda}$. The fitted plots mapping both sine and square waves are shown in \autoref{fig:diff_fit}.

The slope extracted for the sine wave was $m_{\text{sine}} = \sineSlope$, which theoretically correlates to an ultrasonic velocity of \sineFitV. The corresponding slope for the square wave was $m_{\text{square}} = \sqSlope$, yielding a velocity of \sqFitV. Notably, the square wave generated visually sharper and more distinct interference spots, leading to a much higher confidence in its linear fit and distance readings.

\subsection*{Error Estimation}
A prominent systematic error in our optical setup arose from the intrinsic beam profile of the laser. The natural output of our specific laser was pill-shaped rather than a geometrically ideal circular dot. Consequently, the diffracted interference spots inherited this pill-shaped horizontal broadening. This optical distortion significantly complicated the ability to accurately pinpoint the exact center of the spot using the microscope crosshairs, thereby inflating the uncertainty in the measurement of $D_n$. 

Additionally, while the microscope possessed a reliable least count of 0.01 mm, minor mechanical backlash and standard human error in centering the crosshairs on the broadened spots collectively propagate fractional measurement errors through the subsequent sine approximations and velocity calculations.

\section{Conclusion}
The ultrasonic diffraction experiment was successfully conducted to find the velocity of sound in the liquid medium. The ultrasonic velocity was estimated to be \sineFitV\ using the sine wave and \sqFitV\ using the square wave. Consistent with theoretical expectations, the square wave provided enhanced visual clarity of the acoustic grating, proving to be the superior driving signal for accurate peak-detection in this experimental framework.
