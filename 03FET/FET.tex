\section{Aim}
The aim of the experiment is to study the drain and transfer characterstics of an p-channel Junction Field Effect Transistor. 
We shall forward-bias the gate-source junction, reverse-bias the drain-source junction, and use a fixed voltage bias method to construct our setup.

\section{Theory}
A Junction Field Effect Transistor (JFET) is a voltage-controlled semiconductor device in which the drain current ($I_D$) is 
controlled by the gate-to-source voltage ($V_{GS}$). The gate is reverse biased, resulting in very high input impedance.
\subsection*{Drain Characterstics}
Drain characteristics represent the variation of drain current ($I_D$) with drain-to-
source voltage ($V_{DS}$) for different constant values of gate-to-source voltage ($V_{GS}$).
\subsection*{Transfer Characterstics}
Transfer characteristics represent the variation of drain current ($I_D$) with gate-to-
source voltage ($V_{GS}$) at a constant drain-to-source voltage ($V_{DS}$). These
characteristics are useful for determining the transconductance of the JFET.

We start by plotting the characterstics curve from the observation data. We get the expected curves for both the cases as seen in 
\autoref{fig:drain} and \autoref{fig:transfer} for Drain and Transfer characterstics respectively.

For the \autoref{fig:drain}, The x-axis represents the drain-to-source voltage ($V_{DS}$). For a p-channel device, this value is negative. 
At low values of $V_{DS}$, the FET acts like a variable resistor. The drain current $I_D$ rises rapidly and almost linearly as $V_{DS}$ increases. 
As $V_{DS}$ becomes more negative, the current eventually stops rising and levels off. 
In this region, $I_D$ remains relatively constant regardless of changes in $V_{DS}$. 
The highest curve is when $V_{GS} = \qty{0}{\volt}$ (resulting in $I_{DS}$ in the saturation region). 
As you make $V_{GS}$ more positive, the entire curve shifts downward, meaning the saturation current occurs at lower levels.

For the \autoref{fig:transfer}, the control voltage ($V_{GS}$) is positive, and the drain current ($I_D$) magnitude is positive. 
Therefore, the curve is plotted in the first quadrant of a standard Cartesian plane. 
As you apply an increasingly positive voltage to the gate, the depletion region expands, narrowing the conductive channel. 
The specific positive voltage where the channel closes completely and $I_D$ drops to $\qty{0}{\ampere}$ is the pinch-off voltage ($+V_P$). 
The curve is not a straight line; it is a parabola. The relationship is mathematically defined by Shockley's Equation.

Now, we shall move on to calculate some other parameters for the pchannel JFET like the drain resistance ($r_d$), transconductance ($g_m$) and 
amplification factor ($\mu$).

First calculating the drain resistance; Drain resistance is defined as the ratio of a small change in drain-to-source voltage
to the corresponding change in drain current, keeping VGS constant, when the JFET operates in the pinch-off region.
$$ r_d = \left( \frac{\partial V_{DS}}{\partial I_D} \right) $$
We will find the slope for the different pinchoff regions in our drain curve.

We would ignore the value for $r_d$ at $V_{GS} = -1.0$ as it doesn't seem to be in the pinchoff region properly in the observations we have taken. 
So, the final drain resistance we can take average for our other two readings. Thus, $r_d = \qty{6.2744}{\kilo\ohm}$.

Now, calculating the transconductance ($g_m$); Transconductance is defined as the ratio of a small change in drain current to the
corresponding change in gate-to-source voltage, keeping VDS constant.
$$ g_m = \left( \frac{\partial I_D}{\partial V_{GS}} \right) $$

I start by calculating the derivative for all the points on the transfer curve and then finding the maximum value for the transconductance for each $V_{DS}$.

So we can say the actual $g_m$ will be much closer to the actual value so our estimation of $g_m$ is $\gmZeroVDSSixPointZeroTwo \unit{\milli\mho}$.

Now, calculating the amplification factor; Amplification factor is defined as the ratio of a small change in drain-to-source
voltage to the corresponding change in gate-to-source voltage, keeping ID constant.
\begin{align*}
	\mu &= \left( \frac{\partial V_{DS}}{\partial V_{GS}} \right) \\
	\mu & = r_d \times g_m
\end{align*}

So the value is simply just:
\begin{align*}
	\mu &= 6.2744 \times \gmZeroVDSSixPointZeroTwo \\
	    &= \num{129.1459}
\end{align*}
